The key

In the 25+ years I've been playing music I have practiced multiple instruments, but never spent much time on piano. My wife and I recently purchased a new digital piano for use by both ourselves and the kids and I've been having tremendous fun getting to know her ins and outs.

Guitars, whistles, misc

My first and greatest love is the guitar. I started playing classical guitar at age 7 and although I stopped having lessons after about 3 years, I never really stopped playing since. I mostly played whatever I enjoyed listening to, as long as it didn't sound completely out of place on six strings.
I always had a special place in my heart for folk music, in particular celtic folk - traditional tunes from Ireland, Scotland, and Brittany. I started practicing the tin whistle and joined a band where I played guitar and whistles. The other band members were classically trained and encouraged me to learn more about music theory.

Keys in a row

We had an old upright piano at home when I was little, but I can't have spent more than an hour in ten years playing it. To me it lacked the inherent rhythm of strumming chords, and I could not play melody on it with the ease that I had gotten used to on guitar. As a student I had a crummy keyboard, mostly for adding percussion and organ parts to music that I was by this time writing and recording - I never used it to play actual piano pieces.
Now that I'm putting some actual time and effort into learning to play, I'm discovering new things about music. Building and shifting chords on a piano is completely different, yet extremely familiar. Piano chords tend to consist of the 'pure' three or four notes, rather than the typical 4-6 when playing 'cowboy chords' on guitar. It becomes easier to tell apart differences in subsequent chords, and the interplay between chord and melody sounds subtly more profound.

Purity

The digital piano cannot be out of tune. You can play terribly dissonant combinations of notes, but each note individually will always have exactly the correct frequency. Guitars, by contrast, are inherently out of tune. Even if an individual string is tuned to perfection, pressing down that string even with perfect intonation will always produce an ever so slightly out of tune note.
This is very much part of the charm of guitar music, and not something that players should strive to prevent in my opinion. I'm certainly not saying that a piano is objectively a better instrument because of this characteristic, either - and it's not entirely true of acoustic pianos anyway.
It does mean that putting music theory to practice is easier when playing piano than when playing guitar. All of your notes are in a row, clearly contrasting whole notes with sharps and flats. Playing a C major chord on that row of notes is as simple as grabbing each second white note from C until you run out of fingers. Changing to an A minor by shifting your last finger up one, or to an Em by shifting your first finger down one, exposes the relationship between those chords.

No contest

Guitar will always be my first and greatest love. It is expressive in a way that no other instrument could ever match, and it has taught me countless lessons in practice and theory. I'm only able to progress quickly on piano because of all the experience gained on guitar.
Playing piano is teaching me other lessons, broadening my horizon, enriching my experience, and making me a better guitarist in the process.
